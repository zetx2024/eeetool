What is Sample Size?

Sample size refers to the number of individual samples measured or observations used in a survey or experiment. In research, it's the number of subjects, respondents, or observations included in your study.

Why Sample Size Matters

Choosing an appropriate sample size is crucial because:

  • It affects the reliability and validity of your research findings
  • Too small a sample may not represent the population accurately
  • Too large a sample may waste resources without adding value
  • It impacts the statistical power of your study
  • Proper sample size ensures ethical research practices

Key Concept

The sample size is a balance between practical constraints (time, budget) and statistical requirements (precision, confidence). The goal is to select the smallest sample that will provide the most accurate results.

What is a Good Sample Size?

A "good" sample size depends on several factors:

  • Research objectives: Descriptive studies vs. hypothesis testing
  • Population variability: More diverse populations require larger samples
  • Desired precision: Smaller margins of error require larger samples
  • Confidence level: Higher confidence requires larger samples
  • Expected effect size: Smaller expected effects require larger samples

What is a Large Sample Size?

While definitions vary by field:

  • In social sciences: 100-1,000 respondents is common
  • In medical research: Hundreds to thousands may be needed
  • For national surveys: 1,000-2,500 is typical
  • In qualitative research: 15-50 participants may suffice

A sample is "large" when adding more participants doesn't significantly change your results.

How to Calculate Sample Size

Sample Size Formula

The basic formula for calculating sample size (for infinite populations) is:

n = (Z² × p × (1-p)) / e²

Where:

  • n = sample size
  • Z = Z-score (standard score corresponding to your chosen confidence level)
  • p = response distribution (proportion)
  • e = margin of error

For Finite Populations

When you know the population size (N), the adjusted formula is:

n' = n / (1 + ((n - 1) / N))

Z-Score Values

Common confidence levels and their corresponding Z-scores:

  • 99% confidence → Z = 2.576
  • 95% confidence → Z = 1.96
  • 90% confidence → Z = 1.645
  • 80% confidence → Z = 1.282

Key Factors Affecting Sample Size

Confidence Level

How confident you need to be that your results reflect the true population value. Higher confidence requires larger samples.

Margin of Error

The acceptable amount of error in your results. Smaller margins require larger samples.

Population Size

Total size of the group you're studying. Only matters for small populations.

Response Distribution

Expected distribution of responses. 50% is most conservative (requires largest sample).

Sample Size Calculation Examples

Example 1: National Survey

Scenario: Surveying voter preferences for a national election

  • Population: 50 million eligible voters
  • Confidence level: 95% (Z = 1.96)
  • Margin of error: 3%
  • Response distribution: 50% (most conservative)

Calculation: (1.96² × 0.5 × 0.5) / 0.03² = 1,067 respondents

Example 2: University Research

Scenario: Studying student satisfaction at a university

  • Population: 10,000 students
  • Confidence level: 95% (Z = 1.96)
  • Margin of error: 5%
  • Response distribution: 50%

Calculation: First calculate infinite population size: (1.96² × 0.5 × 0.5) / 0.05² = 384. Then adjust for finite population: 384 / (1 + (383/10000)) ≈ 370 students

Example 3: Medical Study

Scenario: Clinical trial for a new medication

  • Population: Patients with specific condition (unknown size)
  • Confidence level: 99% (Z = 2.576)
  • Margin of error: 2%
  • Response distribution: 50%

Calculation: (2.576² × 0.5 × 0.5) / 0.02² = 4,147 participants

Sample Size Best Practices

Planning Your Sample Size

  • Conduct a power analysis for experimental studies to determine minimum sample size needed to detect an effect
  • Consider your research design - different methods require different sample size approaches
  • Account for expected attrition - add 10-20% more participants if you anticipate dropouts
  • Check similar studies in your field for typical sample sizes
  • Balance precision and practicality - don't collect more data than you can properly analyze

Common Sample Size Mistakes to Avoid

Too Small Samples

Results may not be statistically significant or generalizable. Increases risk of Type II errors (false negatives).

Arbitrary Numbers

Choosing sample sizes based on convenience rather than statistical calculation leads to unreliable results.

Ignoring Population Size

For small populations (under 5,000), failing to adjust sample size calculations can lead to oversampling.

Uneven Group Sizes

In comparative studies, unequal group sizes reduce statistical power and can bias results.

Special Considerations

Qualitative Research

Sample size is determined by theoretical saturation (when no new information is obtained) rather than statistical power. Typically 15-50 participants.

Experimental Studies

Focus on statistical power (typically 80% or higher) to detect meaningful effects. Power analysis is essential.
